Faithfulness in Relationships

Based on: 04.12.26 Faithfulness_in_relationships | Series: Ruth

Main Point: God calls us to faithfully love and serve the people He has placed in our lives.

Lesson Introduction

Faithfulness is "the quality of being true, trustworthy, and reliable in all one's dealings with others." In Christian circles, it refers to more than character—it also relates to our acceptance of the gospel and our commitment to Christ as Savior and Lord. Because faithfulness is an aspect of the fruit of the Spirit, it doesn't come naturally, especially during life's trying times. True faithfulness, birthed in us by the Holy Spirit, is a perseverance through every season of life. Such is the example Ruth set for us.

Icebreaker Questions

  • Faithfulness is "the quality of being true, trustworthy, and reliable in all one's dealings with others." Is this a quality our culture values? Think of some recent examples to support your answer.
  • Why is faithfulness an important attribute for the Christ-follower to exhibit?

Naomi & Ruth's Journey

Bethlehem → Moab → Bethlehem

Section 1: Emptied (Ruth 1:1-13)

1Now it came about in the days when the judges governed, that there was a famine in the land. And a certain man of Bethlehem in Judah went to reside in the land of Moab, he and his wife and his two sons. 2The man's name was Elimelech, and his wife's name was Naomi, and the names of his two sons were Mahlon and Chilion, Ephrathites of Bethlehem in Judah. Now they entered the land of Moab and remained there. 3Then Elimelech, Naomi's husband, died; and she was left with her two sons. 4They took for themselves Moabite women as wives; the name of the one was Orpah, and the name of the other was Ruth. And they lived there about ten years. 5Then both Mahlon and Chilion also died, and the woman was bereft of her two children and of her husband.

🏛️ Did You Know? The Time of the Judges
The opening phrase "in the days when the judges governed" is not merely a date stamp — it is a theological warning. The book of Judges closes with one of Scripture's most haunting refrains: "there was no king in Israel; everyone did what was right in his own eyes" (Judg. 21:25). Elimelech's move to Moab is an example of exactly this: doing what seemed right to him rather than trusting God in the land of promise. Bethlehem — ironically, whose name means "house of bread" — was experiencing famine, yet it was still the Promised Land. Leaving it for the pagan land of Moab was an act of practical pragmatism that had devastating consequences.
🏛️ Did You Know? Name Meanings in Ruth 1
Hebrew names in this story carry heavy irony. Elimelech means "My God is king" — yet he left God's land to seek his own provision. Naomi means "pleasant" or "delightful," evoking Psalm 16:6: "the boundary lines have fallen for me in pleasant places." Yet she and her husband were dissatisfied with God's appointed boundaries. Their sons' names — Mahlon and Chilion — appear to be related to the Hebrew words for sickness and mortality respectively, as if the text is hinting from the start at their fate. Ruth likely means "friend" or "companion." Boaz, introduced in 2:1, means "in him is strength."

6Then she arose with her daughters-in-law that she might return from the land of Moab, for she had heard in the land of Moab that the Lord had visited His people in giving them food. 7So she departed from the place where she was, and her two daughters-in-law with her; and they went on the way to return to the land of Judah. 8And Naomi said to her two daughters-in-law, "Go, return each of you to her mother's house. May the Lord deal kindly with you as you have dealt with the dead and with me. 9May the Lord grant that you may find rest, each in the house of her husband." Then she kissed them, and they lifted up their voices and wept. 10And they said to her, "No, but we will surely return with you to your people." 11But Naomi said, "Return, my daughters. Why should you go with me? Have I yet sons in my womb, that they may be your husbands? 12Return, my daughters! Go, for I am too old to have a husband. If I said I have hope, if I should even have a husband tonight and also bear sons, 13would you therefore wait until they were grown? Would you therefore refrain from marrying? No, my daughters; for it is harder for me than for you, for the hand of the Lord has gone forth against me."

🏛️ Did You Know? Levirate Marriage & the Widow's Crisis
Naomi's argument in verses 11–13 references levirate marriage — from the Latin levir, meaning "husband's brother." Ancient Israelite law (Deut. 25:5-10) required that if a married man died without children, his brother was to marry the widow and raise up an heir in the deceased man's name. This protected the widow economically and preserved the family line. Naomi's point: she has no more sons to give, and even if she could conceive tonight, her daughters-in-law couldn't wait 20 years for them to grow up. In a world with no welfare system, no respectable occupations for single women, and no legal protections for widows outside of family, Naomi's situation was genuinely desperate. Her unselfishness in releasing Ruth and Orpah, rather than clinging to them for her own security, is a remarkable act of love.
Lesson Insight: Naomi's Unselfishness
We could hardly have blamed Naomi if she had clung to these young women for her own comfort and survival. Instead, she did the painful, unselfish thing: she told them to go. Holding on to them would have served her interests, not theirs. This is what faithful love sometimes looks like — releasing people to what is genuinely best for them, even at personal cost.

Discussion: The Crisis of Emptiness

  • Without husbands or sons, what crises is Naomi facing? In a male-dominated, pre-welfare age, how important would male relatives be for widows?
  • Given the social problems facing these widows, why do you think Naomi told Ruth and Orpah to return to their families?
  • How did Naomi bless her daughters-in-law? What did her words reveal about her relationship with them?
  • What can we surmise about Naomi, Ruth, and Orpah's character based on this parting conversation?

Section 2: Clung (Ruth 1:14-18)

14And they lifted up their voices and wept again; and Orpah kissed her mother-in-law, but Ruth clung to her. 15Then she said, "Behold, your sister-in-law has gone back to her people and her gods; return after your sister-in-law." 16But Ruth said, "Do not urge me to leave you or turn back from following you; for where you go, I will go, and where you lodge, I will lodge. Your people shall be my people, and your God, my God. 17Where you die, I will die, and there I will be buried. Thus may the Lord do to me, and worse, if anything but death parts you and me." 18When she saw that she was determined to go with her, she said no more to her.

🏛️ Did You Know? "Clung" — A Marriage Word
The Hebrew verb translated "clung" in verse 14 is dabaq — the same word used in Genesis 2:24 to describe the marriage bond: "a man shall leave his father and his mother, and shall cleave [dabaq] to his wife." Ruth's attachment to Naomi is described with the deepest word of human commitment in the Hebrew vocabulary. This is not a casual fondness; it is a covenant bond. The text is quietly signaling something extraordinary about Ruth's loyalty before she even speaks.
🏛️ Did You Know? Ruth's Self-Imprecatory Oath
Ruth's vow in verse 17 — "Thus may the Lord do to me, and worse, if anything but death parts you and me" — is a self-imprecatory oath, a solemn ancient Near Eastern formula in which the speaker invites divine punishment upon themselves if they break their word. It is the most serious form of promise-making in the biblical world. Crucially, Ruth swears this oath in the personal name of Israel's God, Yahweh — not in the name of the Moabite deity Chemosh. This is Ruth's confession of faith: she has abandoned her former gods and cast her entire future on the God of Naomi, sight unseen. The commitment was total — covering journey, dwelling, nationality, religion, death, and burial.
Lesson Insight: Orpah Is Not the Villain
It is easy to read Orpah as the "bad" daughter-in-law, but the text does not support this. Orpah also loved Naomi, wept over her, and followed Naomi's own sensible advice. She did nothing wrong. The point is not that Orpah failed — it is that Ruth exceeded all reasonable expectation. Ruth's faithfulness was extraordinary, not a baseline requirement. This matters when we apply the story: the call to faithfulness may go well beyond what anyone could demand of us.

Discussion: The Commitment

  • Orpah returns to her family while Ruth remains with Naomi. Was one action a better demonstration of faithfulness than the other? Explain your thoughts.
  • How far was Ruth willing to go in her loyalty and faithfulness to Naomi?
  • What made Ruth's verbal commitment so powerful?
  • Although Ruth 1:16-17 is used in wedding vows today, it was originally a daughter-in-law's commitment to her mother-in-law. How do these expressions of love reflect a choice to sacrifice?

Section 3: Return & Initiative (Ruth 1:19–2:3)

19So they both went until they came to Bethlehem. And when they had come to Bethlehem, all the city was stirred because of them, and the women said, "Is this Naomi?" 20She said to them, "Do not call me Naomi; call me Mara, for the Almighty has dealt very bitterly with me. 21I went out full, but the Lord has brought me back empty. Why do you call me Naomi, since the Lord has witnessed against me and the Almighty has afflicted me?" 22So Naomi returned, and with her Ruth the Moabitess, her daughter-in-law, who returned from the land of Moab. And they came to Bethlehem at the beginning of barley harvest.

🏛️ Did You Know? Naomi / Mara & the Waters of Marah
"Naomi" means "pleasant" or "delightful." "Mara" means "bitter." The connection is deliberate: in Exodus 15:23, the Israelites journeying out of Egypt came to a place called Marah — bitter water they could not drink — and complained against the Lord. Naomi's heart was in a similar place: physically back in Bethlehem, but spiritually bitter and turned against God. Yet the parallel with Marah also contains seeds of hope. God healed the bitter water at Marah (Ex. 15:25). The reader is invited to wonder: will He do the same for Naomi? Naomi's lament, though raw and honest, does not mean she has abandoned faith — the Psalms are full of similar cries (Pss. 22:1; 43:2; 74:1). Lament is not the opposite of faith; it is often an expression of it.
Lesson Insight: Both Ruth and Naomi Confess God's Sovereignty
Notice that both women, in their very different emotional states, ascribe control of their situation to God. Ruth swears her oath by Yahweh (v.17). Naomi protests that "the Almighty has dealt very bitterly with me" (v.20) and "the hand of the Lord has gone forth against me" (v.13). Both confess God's sovereign control of events — one in trust, one in grief. Neither denies His involvement. The text doesn't rebuke Naomi for her lament; it records it honestly. God can handle our bitterness.

1Now Naomi had a kinsman of her husband, a man of great wealth, of the family of Elimelech, whose name was Boaz. 2And Ruth the Moabitess said to Naomi, "Please let me go to the field and glean among the ears of grain after one in whose sight I may find favor." And she said to her, "Go, my daughter." 3So she departed and went and gleaned in the field after the reapers; and she happened to come to the portion of the field belonging to Boaz, who was of the family of Elimelech.

🏛️ Did You Know? Gleaning — God's Welfare Law
Gleaning was Israel's divinely mandated poverty relief system. Leviticus 19:9-10 required landowners to leave the edges of their fields unharvested and not to pick up grain that fell to the ground — this was to be left for the poor and the foreigner. It was not a handout; it required real physical labor, following behind the harvesters in the heat of the day, stooping repeatedly to pick up individual stalks. Ruth would also have had to seek a field whose owner would actually allow her, a foreign woman, to glean there — not all would. Her willingness to do whatever was necessary, however exhausting or humbling, to provide for Naomi is a concrete expression of the faithfulness she verbally committed to in chapter 1.
🏛️ Did You Know? "She Happened to Come" — Providence
The phrase in 2:3 translated "she happened to come" (or in some versions, "as it turned out") uses Hebrew idiom that signals what appears to be random chance — but the author is winking at the reader. In the theology of Ruth, there are no coincidences. The God who is sovereign over famines, deaths, and the direction of a widow's feet is equally sovereign over which field a gleaner wanders into. Ruth "happened" to land in the field of the one man in Bethlehem who was a kinsman-redeemer of Naomi's family. The narrator's dry understatement is one of the Bible's finest literary touches.

Discussion: Bitterness, Initiative & Providence

  • Both Ruth (vv. 16-17) and Naomi (vv. 20-21) confess God's sovereign control of events, each in her own way. What truth does each convey of God? Of themselves? Of their perseverance through trial?
  • What was Ruth willing to do to provide food for Naomi?
  • Gleaning was an arduous task involving great physical discomfort without producing much fruit. How did her commitment demonstrate faithfulness?

Application & Prayer

  • How does faithfulness in relationships require us to invest emotionally, socially, physically, mentally, and spiritually?
